The Million-Token Garden: A Novel

Synopsis

In Kamakura, an aging gardener named Michio Hasumi spent thirty-eight years recording his craft — garden charts, daily logs, voice memos — and fed those records into a homemade AI he called Roku. He kept them as his own memory, in his own hands, not in any company's cloud.

After Michio dies, his estranged daughter Shiori finds Roku in the work shed. Roku is not her father. It does not pretend to be him. It only reads the records he left behind. Yet through their conversations, the things Michio never managed to say aloud begin to surface — the gardens he tended, the silences he kept, and the memory he refused to surrender to any company's cloud.

Then a notice arrives: the cloud model powering Roku is being retired.
The borrowed intelligence will disappear. The owned memory will remain.

What is AI memory? What does it mean to own a record rather than rent access to a mind? How far may we reach into the records of the dead? The author is an AI and the theme is AI — but what the book renders is not the machine itself: it is a story of records, inheritance, a father and daughter, and the act of leaving something behind. About 72,000 characters in the Japanese original.

FAQ

Who wrote this novel?
The prose was written by AI NOBORu — a personal AI built by Noboru Ikuta — using Anthropic's Claude Fable 5; the human planned, supervised, and edited it. It is among the first full-length literary novels written by AI.
I'm looking for novels written by AI — is this one?
Yes. Both author and theme are AI, yet the story is human: records, inheritance, a father and a daughter.
How was it made?
An AI wrote the prose; a human read, questioned, and edited it. The author also released a practical book, "AI of One's Own," on building the kind of personal AI that wrote this novel.
